This report covers the simulation excercize from the first part of the statistical inference course project. The report does not show the full R code. The complete reproduceable code can be found at my github account at https://github.com/zoharpeleg/Statistical-Inference-Course-Project

In this excercise I have investigated the properties of the distribution of the mean of 40 random exponential variables, using R simulation of the exponential distribution. The simulation is using the rexp(n,lambda), where lambda is the rate parameter, and n is the sample size. I used lambda=0.2 and n=40 for all simulations,as required by the project instructions. In order to get an idea about the distribution of the sample’s properties, I have repeated the simulation for a large number of times (nosim=1000).

Setting the simulation parameters:

lambda<-0.2 ; n<-40 ; nosim<-1000 ; set.seed(9876543)

The simulated samples are stored in a matrix, where each row contains one sample of 40 random exponential variables. The mean and standard-deviation were calculated for each sample, and stored in two vectors - meanx, and sdx respectively:

x<-matrix(rexp(n*nosim,lambda),nosim) #nosim samples of n observations
meanx<-apply(x, 1, mean)              #the means of all samples        
sdx<-apply(x, 1, sd)                  #the std-deviations of all samples
  1. Showing where the distribution is centered at, and comparing it to the theoretical center of the distribution:

The average mean of all the samples is:

mean(meanx) 
## [1] 4.993

compared with the theoretical mean of the exponential distribution, which is \(\mu=1/\lambda=5\)

  1. Showing how variable the sample mean is, and comparing it to the theoretical variance of the distribution:

The calculated variance of the sample means is:

var(meanx)
## [1] 0.6444

compared with the theoretical variance of the sample mean, for sample-size of n, which is
\(s^2=\sigma^2/n=(1/\lambda)^2/n=5^2/40=0.625\)

  1. Showing that the distribution is approximately normal:
  1. By plotting a normalized histogram of the sample means, compared to the density function curve of normal distribution of \(\mu=1/\lambda\) and \(\sigma=1/\lambda/\sqrt{n}\):
hist(meanx, breaks=40, col="green", freq=F, main="Histogram of meanx vs normal curve")  #plot histogram
xaxis<-seq(min(meanx),max(meanx),length=100) #prepare the x-axis 
lines(xaxis,
      dnorm(xaxis,1/lambda,sd=1/lambda/sqrt(n)),
      type="l", col="red", lwd=2)  #add ref normal curve

  1. By plotting a Q-Q plot of the sample means compared to random normal sample with the same expected parameters as above:
qqplot(rnorm(1000,1/lambda,sd=1/lambda/sqrt(n)),meanx, pch=20, cex = .5, col="black", main="Q-Q plot: meanx vs. normal dist.", xlab="normal")
abline(0,1, col = "red",lwd=1,lty=2)

  1. Evaluation of the coverage of the confidence interval for \(1/\lambda\) using \(\overline{X}\pm 1.96*s/\sqrt{n}\):

With the sample’s mean and standard deviation, I have calculated the interval’s lower limit and upper limit for each sample:

ul<-meanx+sdx*1.96/sqrt(n) #upper limit vector
ll<-meanx-sdx*1.96/sqrt(n) #lower limit vector

Now it is possible to evaluate the percentage of good intervals, which contain the mean=5, as expected:

paste(mean(1/lambda<ul&1/lambda>ll)*100,"% of the intervals contained the population mean",sep="")
## [1] "92.2% of the intervals contained the population mean"

Repeating the set many times, I have found that the coverage was consistently about 92%. I tried various sample sizes and figured out that when the sample size is increased from 40 to 400, the coverage goes up to 95%. My conclusion is that a sample size of 40 exponentials(0.2), is not sufficiently large for 95% confidence by CLT.

I also tried replacing the normal CLT interval with t-interval, by replacing the 1.96 with qt(0.975,n-1) = 2.0227. The coverage increased to about 93%, which is about 0.5%-1% improvement.

I am adding an interesting view of the distribution of the calculated intervals compared to the population’s mean, by plotting each sample’s interval as (x=ll; y=ul):

plot(ll,ul, pch = 20, cex = .5)
abline(v=5,col=3,lty=3)
abline(h=5,col=3,lty=3)

The point (5,5) represents the ultimate interval (ul=ll=5). All the points in the upper-left rectangle represent good intervals which contain the population mean \(1/\lambda\) (ll<5 and ul>5). The points in the upper-right area are the upper tail, in which the interval’s lower limit is greater than 5, and the lower-left area is the lower tail, in which the interval’s upper limit is smaller than 5.
